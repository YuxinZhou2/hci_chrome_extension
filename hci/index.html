The Problem

From our observations, we noticed that the biggest distraction for students during moments that require intense focus, such as when they are studying or listening to lectures, are internet-connected laptops. More specifically, the problem resides in the convenience and subtlety that using a web browser on the laptop, especially for distraction and entertainment, provides. Indeed, because the majority of a student’s work needs the web, it is too convenient and effortless for them to lose focus by opening a new Facebook “tab” and scrolling through their news feed, to swipe left to a new window showing episodes of “The Office”, or to wander off on countless forms of entertainment and social media.

This problem is further amplified by the subtlety of consuming entertainment or going on social media during lectures. In fact, when using a phone, the teacher can’t easily tell if the student is using his laptop to follow along with the lecture or to consume mundane web media. Therefore, the student doesn’t have any friction, in the form of guilt from not paying attention to the teacher, to go on Facebook and talk with his friends while the teacher is giving a lecture.

Moreover, the student rarely notices that they are getting distracted for hours at a time. Most of the time, whenever the students opens a new tab on Instagram or Facebook, they are doing it unconsciously. In fact whenever a task gets boring or mentally taxing, the student is immediately inclined to open his favorite social website to get alter his emotions, which sometimes ends up in the student wasting hours throughout the day. However, in most cases, the student is completely aware that this behavior is a waste of time. They simply don’t realize it in the moment. Just like Tristan Harris, a design ethicist at Google, said in his TED talk “How a handful of tech companies control billions of minds every day”: “Right now it’s as if all of our technology is basically only asking our lizard brain what’s the best way to just impulsively get you to do the next tiniest thing with your time, instead of asking you in your life what we would be most time well spent for you.” In the exact moment between focused work and opening a new tab for distraction, the student doesn’t have the built-in reflex to pause and think about what they’re getting themselves into, or why they feel the absolute need to distract themselves in that moment. The web is designed to provide them incentive to mindlessly wander off from their state of focus.

Figure 1: Tristan Harris's Ted Talk.


In “A call to Minimize Distraction” , Harris explains that “When access to the next hit is too frictionless, we lose the ability to consider before acting. [...] When we lose that moment to consider before acting on our impulses we lose what sets us apart from thinking humans.” That “pause” is what gives use the ability to ponder, and it is what we want to give back to people. Through our product, in the form of a web plugin, we will insert a layer of friction between the state of focus and the state of distraction by prompting the user with the question “Why?” whenever they are about to open a new tab recognized as being distracting and unproductive. It is important to notice that our goal is not to block the user from accessing a distraction, but to let the user think and evaluate his web usage for himself. We believe that this layer, which gives the user a second to think about their behavior, is the missing key required for our users to allow themselves to remain focused on their work.

Use Case Scenarios


Use case 1

Angela comes back from school and gets ready to start studying. She opens her laptop and the first page she opens is Facebook. The plugin that she has already installed redirects her to the question “Why?”. She pauses and squints. She doesn’t know why. She proceeds on reading the three options that are offered to her. Is it because it’s a priority? No, her midterm is her priority. Is it because she NEEDS it? No, she needs to study. Or because it’s productive? She closes the page.

Explanation: In this use case, upon being redirected to the question “Why?” Angela had time to realized by herself that she did not have valid reasons to go on Facebook and that she had more important things to do instead. This is of course the main success scenario. The following use cases will address situations where it does not go as planned.


Use case 2

Angela is in class and find herself so bored by the lecture that she started daydreaming. Instead, she decides to go on her social media (might as well, since she is not paying attention anyways). She already knows that she is going to be prompted with the question “Why?”. She picks a random choice and accesses her social media.

Explanation: Even though Angela was able to access a source of distraction, the important thing is that she made a mindful decision. She is also less likely to turn off completely the plugin because it still gives her access to what she wants. It’s something that adds just enough friction so that it doesn’t become annoying. It’s something that can be integrated on the long-term, and not only activated during desperate moments such as midterms and finals season.


Use case 3

Nicolas occasionally wakes up early in the morning. Every time he does, he spends the entire time watching youtube in his bed until he has to go to class. This morning, upon waking up, still groggy, he directly reached for his laptop and opens up Youtube. His eyes adjusting from the brightness of his screen, he suddenly sees a big “Why?” displayed. He pauses, murmurs “Why?” and answers himself with “because I want to”. More awake now, he reads the three options and realizes that he needs to choose one in order to access Youtube. Is it his priority? No, he could go on a morning run. Is it because he NEEDS it? No, there’s many more important things he needs, like eating breakfast, catching up on missed lectures, washing two weeks old dishes. Is it because it is productive? Sleeping would probably be more productive than that. He closes his laptop and goes back to sleep.

Explanation:In this use case, Nicolas realizes that he could be doing something better than going on Youtube. He was the one controlling the choices on his menus (“How Technology is Hijacking Your mind - From a Magician and a Google Design Ethicist”, Tristan Harris).


Use case 4

Joseph gets to work early to workout in the building’s gym. After his workout, he eats breakfast with his coworkers and gets to work. During work hours he rarely gets distracted as him and his team all sit next to each other in an open space; it would make him look bad and he has deadlines to meet. After work, he goes home, cooks supperm prepares his lunch for the next day and heads to the library for the rest of the night. His goal for the night is to finish writing the lab report for his recent experiment as he knows he won’t have time for the rest of the week. He shuts down his cellphone, open his laptop and starts working. He goes on the web and searches for references to support claims he’s making in his report. He opens one tab after the other, reads numerous articles, goes back and forth between his browser and his document, and suddenly opens the WhatsApp webpage. Usually he ends up spending a considerable amount of time chatting with his friends, before realizing it. He always ends up asking himself “How did I even got here?” However this time, he was prompted with the question “Why?” and right away, closed his browser.

Explanation: In this case, for a productivity junky like Joseph, having that moment of friction puts him back on track without him even needing to read the three options.


Related Products


Alternative 1: BlockSite, and similar website blockers


Description: BlockSite is a very popular chrome extension (and also mobile app) that can block the user from opening websites that they select themselves in the settings. They can set the blocking behaviour to follow a schedule, and add a layer of security with a password which they would have to provide to unblock websites.

Figure 3: The Chrome extension "BlockSite" blocking facebook.com


Similarities: BlockSite provides a similar layer of friction to our product by preventing the user from accessing a distracting website immediately

Limitations: BlockSite’s layer of friction is very lackluster since it can be easily bypassed by entering a password. Even if the password is set to be extremely long, the user can simply delete the extension or reset their password through their email. Furthermore, the extension doesn’t give the user any insight on their behavior or why they want to access blocked websites. It is merely a substitute for self-discipline, and leaves the user susceptible to distractions on devices that don’t have this extension downloaded. It also takes away the student’s sense of freedom by blocking them from doing what they feel like they want. This is a sure way to irritate the user and make them delete the extension in the long-term.


Alternative 2: Forest, and other rewarding substitutes


Description: Forest is a Chrome extension (and also mobile app) that allows the user to stay focused by growing a virtual garden of trees for 25 minutes at a time. When the user wants to stay focused, they plant a tree. The tree grows for as long as they work and fully grows after 25 minutes of uninterrupted work. However, when a user accesses a website on the blacklist, the tree dies.

Similarities: Much like our proposal, this extension serves as psychological friction layer between the user and the distracting website. Whenever the user wants to access a blacklisted website, the pain of losing their work (of growing the tree) ideally overcomes their urge, and they remain focused

Limitations: Although this extension uses a similar layer of friction as our product (i.e. psychological), it doesn’t provide the user with any insight on their behavior, much like BlockSite. Rewarding the user for not getting distracted is a nice substitute for their general lack of focus on the web. However, this extension doesn’t provide the user the same benefit of knowing why they behave this way. It doesn’t allow them to wonder why they opened a new tab on Facebook and let their trees die. It simply makes them feel sad whenever they get distracted, in hopes that they will eventually stop distracting themselves. Instead, our extension will allow the user to ponder on their behavior, and decide for themselves if they are going on a distracting website mindfully or mindlessly.


Comparing Products

There are a plethora of extensions and plugins that allow the user to either prevent themselves from accessing websites (i.e. Blocksite) or help themselves provide motivation for time spent off distracting websites (i.e. Forest). These extensions generally help the user reduce their time spent on distracting websites, as well as the frequency at which they access websites. However, they all have a major drawback. They are all short term solutions. They don’t provide the user with any insight on their distracted behavior or any reason for the user to question their patterns whenever they end up on a distracting website.

For example, Blocksite, although convenient at preventing the Nicolas from accessing entertainment websites altogether, quickly becomes irritating when Nicolas is in a distracted state and wants to get a quick rush from scrolling through social media. When the user is in this state, they are vulnerable to delete the extension altogether and never install it again. On the other hand, our extension allows the user to access whatever website they want, whenever they want, but instead simply allows the user to question why they want to go on a particular website, giving them just a moment to think about their behavior and giving them the chance to correct it if they want to. We believe that this sense of freedom is crucial for the long term usage of an extension that is meant to correct the distracted behavior altogether.

Moreover, our extension is superior to extensions that reward the user for time spent off of distracting websites, like Forest, because it allows the user to learn and improve their focus based on their patterns of failure, rather than simply substituting the reward of going on social media by another virtual reward (such as trees). Instead of attempting to lure Angela towards a virtual reward, our extension will allow her to develop their self-reflection skills and make her aware of her web consumption whenever she opens a new tab. Since she already knows that going on these websites is going to waste her precious work time, she will likely make the decision to avoid going on them whenever she is prompted of the consciousness of her act. In the long-run, this self-reflection will allow the user to have the reflex of being mindful, and not open new tabs unconsciously, even on devices that don’t have this plugin installed.


Designing at a high-level


Figure 5: High-level diagram of the Chrome Extension "Mindful"


The design of our extension would be pretty simple. We would need to build a Chrome extension, as well as a database to hold the user data, as demonstrated on the diagram. Since most computer users already have the Chrome Browser installed, the user would simply need to install the extension on their device to use it. When they install it, they would set their settings (i.e. blocked websites), which would be stored in the database. Then, whenever they want to access one of these websites through Chrome, the extension would detect if the website is blacklisted, and if so, would prompt the user the question "Why?, before redirecting them to the page they wanted to access.


Justifying feasibility

Although this extension will be a challenge to build, we believe that our team has the appropriate skills to take on this challenge. We know for a fact that building such an extension is feasible to begin with based on BlockSite, which is already able to redirect the user to another web page. Furthermore, Chrome provides developers with many APIs and extensive documentation, giving us a lot of resources to learn from.

The estimate required time effort is at least 30 hours of work per person. Since we have never built a chrome extension before, a big part of the time will be dedicated to researching and learning how this technology works.

To successfully build this extension, we will need UI/UX, frontend development and backend development skills. It will also require someone proficient in the programming languages used for this project, which are Javascript, HTML/CSS, MongoDB and Node.js. Both of our team members have sufficient knowledge in these technologies as Software Engineering students.
