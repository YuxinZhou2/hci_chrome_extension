Testing


Summary

In summary, we completed all pre and post test questionnaires that will be used to analyse the evaluation goals in the “Results and Analysis” section of this document, as well as all data that was required to analyze the usability goals of this system. In brief, the error rate for the tasks in the testing phase was less than 1 per task on average, and all users spent less than 3 minutes on average on all the tasks. Furthermore, the users were almost all satisfied with the look and feel of the application, as well as its purpose and use cases, which were gathered from the Pre and Post test questionnaire. These will be used to evaluate the Nielsen’s heuristics later on. You can find elaborate results in these documents below:

Similarities and Differences

Similarities

Both parents that were tested had problems with the same components, such as creating an account as well as logging out. The same was noticed for the children, where logging in and out were both easy, but both didn’t really understand what their dashboard meant.

Differences

How they reacted faced with the obstacle was different. For example, Zahra was used to spend time on her phone, especially on polished applications like Facebook and Instagram, therefore she had a much higher expectation for TimeTken. She expected the application to work perfectly and would easily get frustrated. On the other hand, Catherine was more lenient and would poke around and ask questions when she didn’t understand a function. As for children, the children’s predisposition to mobile application significantly affected their usability tests. For example, Adrian was already very addicted to is screen, so he didn’t care about using this application. On the other hand, emily was more open to understanding and using the application.

Usability Issues by Priority

Usability issue Severity Analysis
The app does not display information effectively to the user. High As observed during user testing, the information in the application is not self-explanatory. A lot of jargon like “TT”, “Mins left”, “Progress”, “600/1500” without units, etc, were not explained in the app and many of the users we tested could not understand how to understand the data they were presented.
Children can’t remember their passwords High Adrian couldn’t remember his password to login and had to ask the evaluator to remind him what his password was. This could render the entire app useless to the children, and if they can’t see their progress, they wouldn’t use the app at all. An auto-login feature should exist, or no passwords for child account.
The account creation for parents and children is misleading Medium The account creating page affords creating a “child” account as well as a parent account, but the distinction between the two is misleading and frustrating, as observed by both parents that were tested.
User manual is not tailored for children Medium The user manual is mainly addressing the parents of the application. The children in our tests could not understand the vocabulary in the manual if they needed help with the application. It would be likely that the parent would have to explain to the children how to use the application, which is not efficient.
Logout was hard to navigate for parents. Low Parents like Zahra were frustrated at the fact that there weren’t any logout button to log out of the application, just like how they’re used to in popular apps like Instagram and facebook.


Test Plan Critique

Overall, the test plan was effective at gathering data that could be used to determine if the usability goals and the usability heuristics from Nielsen’s list were met.For example, it was very easy to determine if the time criteria, number of errors, and visual appeal goals of the application were met through the usability evaluation plan proposed. However, our team quickly noticed that we there was a major component missing to the test plan. In fact, the user population of the test plan does not include parents, which would use the app the most. It only specifically targets children. Therefore, in order to correctly measure the goals and heuristics, we decided to test it on actual parents. We determined that this was extremely important because the parents are the one thats are mostly targeted by the Nielsen’s heuristics proposed, as well as being the main users of this application.

Furthermore, we noticed that the vocabulary in the children questionnaires was too complicated for a child to be able to fill it out on his own, and many questions did not serve any purpose to test the usability goals, such as "How strictly would you monitor your hypothetical child's screen time?” “How strictly do you currently monitor/restrict your screen usage?” and “How strictly did you monitor/restrict you screen usage as a young teen or child?”. In fact, we created completely new Pre (https://forms.gle/J19XFkjP1EFSjQ9VA) and Post (https://forms.gle/H9tFXgHbDZwXTpn28) questionnaires to address the usability goals more efficiently. The test script was also too complicated to address children, therefore we had to use a boiled down version which was more suited to their vocabulary.

Other minor improvements include replacing the “start time” and “end time” of the benchmark tasks with a “time elapsed” heuristic, which made it easier to collect data with a stopwatch. Moreover, we included an option to make 0 errors in the data collection sheet provided, because sometimes users did not make any errors, and this should be recorded in order to effectively represent if the usability heuristics are met. Lastly, we had to determine that 8/10 would be a good score for the visual usability goal, since no quantitative goal was provided by the team, although it required it.



Design Critique


Below, we've written our design suggestions for each of the usability issues mentioned above.

Usability issue Severity Recomendation
The app does not display information effectively to the user. High Since children don’t have a large vocabulary and don’t know about jargon. Hence instead of writing, we suggest adding images.
  1. For the progress, instead of displaying a percentage number, a progress bar would be more meaningful.
  2. For the amount of tokens, writing “500 out of 1800” would be clearer than “500/1800”. Instead of “TT” as units, the logo of a coin with TT on it would suffice. If “500 ouf of 1800” is not aesthetically pleasing, then many other options are possible, such as having a progress bar, or having “GOAL: 1800” at the top center of the page and “Your coins: 500” after.
Children can’t remember their passwords High Although phones are capable of storing passwords, we won’t make the assumption that the child knows how it works. An easy solution would be to give the parents access to the passwords such that if the child forgets, the parent can remind him.
The account creation for parents and children is misleading Medium
  1. Instead of a toggle button to choose which account the user is signing up for, two checkboxes with “I am a parent” and “I am a child” will allow the user to make a choice and obtain a confirmation.
  2. Since children had trouble finding the create account button:
    1. make the login page also a signup page
    2. Or bring the login in button and the “create account” text closer to the login fields.
User manual is not tailored for children Medium
  1. A straightforward option would be to write a user manual that is tailored for children by using easy vocabulary and images.
  2. Another way that would be a lot more effective, is to directly incorporate it into the app.
    1. An app tour explaining everything when the child first creates its account.
    2. Or, "?” button at the top left.
Logout was hard to navigate for parents. Low
  1. Add a logout button just like the one for the child's account.
  2. If the team doesn’t find it aesthetically pleasing, then, Add a “settings” icon at the top left, and in the settings page, add a logout button.
